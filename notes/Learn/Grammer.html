Grammer

What makes a simple sentence?

  • A sentence need 3 things:
    • Subject
    • Verb
    • It has to make sense together
  • Example:
    • I went home.
      • Subject: home
      • Verb: went
    • The teacher loves to teach.
      • Subject: Teacher
      • Verb: teach
    • The students work hard.

Parts of Speech

  • Verb Action words: go, play, run, eat, laugh.
  • None Thing or a place like: person, ball, dog, book, course, river, london.
  • Adverb A word that describes a verb(action) like: nice, fast, slow, good, bad.
  • Adjective A word describing a none(thing or place) like: nice fast, slow good, bad.

  • Types of Conjunctions:
    • Co-ordinating

      • Words: after, although, as, as if, as long as, as much as, as soon as, as though, because, before, even if, even though, if, in case, in order that, lest, now that, once, only if, provided (that), since, so that, that, though, till, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whereas, wherever, whether, while

Relating appositives

Appositives are nouns or noun phrases that are placed next to another noun or noun phrase (the antecedent) to provide additional, identifying, or explanatory information about it.

Grammar Symbols

  • Comma (,)
    • Used in coordinating conjunctions.
      • Example: grammar is interesting, but grammar is difficult.
    • To attach dependent clause, use a comma if it comes before the independent clause.
      • Example: correct, because it's foundational for our communication.
    • If in structure Dependent clause (subordinating conjunction) comes first then independent clause.
      • Example: although important, grammar is not easy.
    • Rules:
      1. Use a comma before coordinating conjunction when connecting two independent clauses.
        • Example: Alex likes cake, and he likes chocolate too.
      2. Relating appositives (renaming).
        • Example:
          • Alex, like any human being, loves cake.
          • Grammar, like most subjects, is difficult, and it is helpful.
      3. Creating a list.
        • Example: we saw tigers, lions, cheetahs and bears.
      4. After introductory adverbs.
        • Example:
          • Finally, Alex got to eat cake, and he was happy.
          • Lastly, they said, ‚Äúgood job on the project.‚Äù
      5. Attributing quotes: he said, "xyz".
        • Another attribution example (comma inside quotes): "xyz", he said.
      6. If the first word of a sentence is yes or no.
        • Example: yes, he said.
      7. If you are directly addressing someone.
        • Example: Alex, can you please help with xyz?
      8. Two adjectives that modify the same noun.
        • Example: Alex is a nice, friendly instructor.
      9. Offsetting negation.
        • Example: Alex is friendly, not a mean instructor.
      10. After a dependent clause that starts a sentence.
        • Example: when i eat cake, I am happy.
  • Semi-colon(;)
    • Joining related independent clauses.
      • Example: to succeed in a career, you need long-term focus; also need daily focus.
    • With conjunctive adverbs.
      • Example: This software isn't useful enough; moreover, it is very slow and clunky.
    • Use with transition phrases.
      • On the other hand, even so, in addition, as a result, as well as, which raises another interesting point.
      • Example: This employee isn't very experienced; on the other hand, they work very hard.
    • To divide items of a list if the list is long or has internal punctuation.
      • Example:
        • These are the cities: London, England; London, USA; Paris, France; Rome, Italy.
        • Professional skill include writing, presenting, and being organized; Habits to avoid are being disorganized, late, and not paying attention to detail.
    • Example:
      • Grammar is useful; grammar is difficult. (correct, because both are independent sentences).
      • Grammar is useful; also difficult. (incorrect, because second phase is dependent clause).
  • Colon (:)
    • Use with a list.
      • Don't capitalize when introducing a list.
      • Example: I specialize in subject: business, marketing, and productivity.
        • Without colon will be: I specialize in business, marketing, and productivity.
    • Don't capitalize the first word after the colon unless word is a title of something.
      • Example:
        • I love one city: London.
        • I love on kind of a pet: dogs.
    • Similar to the use of semicolon, but when the send part explains the first part.
      • Example:
        • I have no time to get read: the train leaves shortly.
        • I am learning grammar: I write a lot at my job.
        • I am having to learn grammar: I write a lot at my job.
    • When emphasizing the last word in a sentence.
      • Example:
        • After thinking about this my whole life, I decided on my favorite food: cake.
        • He said that there is one professional skill that is more important then any other: good communication.
          • Without colon will be: He said that good communication is most important professional skill.
    • When two or more sentences follow the colon.
      • Example:
        • He did two great thing: He ate cake. Then, he washed the dishes.
        • One skill turned out to be the most important: The ability to write well was one skill that was constantly useful. I used communication all the time.
  • Hyphens (-)
    • They are used for Compound Modifiers. When connecting words to function like on word (adjective).
    • Most of them end with "ed".
      • Examples: hand-painted, short-lived, spine-tingling, quick-witted.
    • In numbers 21-99 when the numbers are spelled out.
      • Example: Twenty-five
    • Words high and low: high-Flying, high-quality, low-frequency.
    • With prefixes like self, all, ex.
      • Example: Self-driving cars, all-consuming, ex-employee.
    • With fraction like quarter-pound, half-way, tenth-of-a-meter.
    • Compound adjective with numbers like 20th-century history, 5-minute break, 10-year.
    • When use past-tense verb like world-traveled, Boston-based, protein-powered.
    • When not to use a hyphen:
      • When the first word ends in "ly." Like newly created, quickly written.
    • When the compound comes after the noun it refer to, you should leave the hyphen out.
      • Example:
        • This is high-quality book.
        • This book is high quality.
    • Words that compound with others
      • check-in
      • merry-go-round
      • father-in-law
      • mother-in-law
      • over-the-counter
      • state-of-the-art
      • up-to-date

  • Quotation Marks ("")

    • Use when: quoting people, test or implying alternate word meaning.

    • No Quotes if you pasting a of text.

    • Only capitalize if you quote a whole sentence.

      • Example:
        • He wrote, "This is great."
        • He wrote, "great."

    • Commas and periods go inside the quotes.

      • Example:
        • He said, "It was great."
        • John asked, "Will this job be difficult?"

    • Dashes, colons, and semicolon go outside quotes.

      • Example: He said, "This was great." - But didn't proceed accordingly.

    • Use it for referencing a direct quote. Not for approximate quotes.

  • Question Mark (?)

    • Use when asking questions.
      • Example:
        • Can you please look at his report?
        • It it possible to get your feedback?
        • I'll make a dentist appointment during work hours, ok?
    • It the question is in the beginning, put the "?" when it ends.
      • Example: is this the right strategy? she wondered.
    • Capitalization and new "?" in a series of questions.
      • Example: I have to create a strategy. Increase spending? Decrease? Stay put?
    • No question mark in statement using indirect questions.
      • Example:
        • I wondered whether he is doing his job well.
        • I kept asking myself how is this possible.
    • Example:
      • Did John ask "Will this job be difficult?"?
      • "Will this job be difficult?" - John asked.
