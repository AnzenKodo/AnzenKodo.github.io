What is Cybercrime?

  • Cybercrime refers to illegal activities that involve computers, the internet, or network devices.
  • Cybercriminals commit identity theft, initiate phishing scams, spread malware, and engage in other activities that can leave a significant financial and social impact on governments, businesses, and individuals.
  • Cybercrime is advancing with technology, adapting to evade existing defenses.
  • The major categories of cybercrimes include attacks caused by cybercriminals, which can include damage to the brand's reputation, legal consequences of a data breach, and loss of sensitive data.
  • Cybercrime is a global problem, and it is difficult to prosecute cybercriminals because they can operate from anywhere in the world.

Types of Cybercrime:

What is ethical hacking?

  • Ethical hacking involves an authorized attempt to gain access to computer systems, applications, or data by duplicating the strategies and methods that would be used by a malicious hacker.
  • The primary goal of ethical hacking is to identify security vulnerabilities that can be addressed and resolved before a malicious hacker can exploit them.
  • Ethical hackers are security experts who perform security assessments to improve an organization's security posture.

Explain types of Hacking

  1. Website hacking: This involves unauthorized access to a website's server or database to steal sensitive information or deface the website.
  2. Computer hacking: This involves unauthorized access to a computer system to steal sensitive information or damage the system.
  3. Network hacking: This involves unauthorized access to a network to steal sensitive information or disrupt network operations.
  4. Email hacking: This involves unauthorized access to an email account to steal sensitive information or send spam emails.
  5. Password hacking: This involves unauthorized access to a password-protected system or account by cracking or guessing the password.

Explain classification of cybercrime

  1. Cybercrime against individuals: This includes crimes such as cyberstalking, cyber harassment, identity theft, and phishing scams that target individuals.
  2. Cybercrime against property: This includes crimes such as credit card fraud, intellectual property theft, and internet time theft that target property.
  3. Cybercrime against organizations: This includes crimes such as unauthorized access to computer systems, denial of service attacks, and data diddling that target organizations.
  4. Cybercrime against society: This includes crimes such as cyber terrorism, web jacking, and forgery that target society at large.

How to prevent cybercrime?

  1. Keep your security software up to date: Having the latest security software goes a long way toward protecting against viruses, malware, and other online threats.
  2. Use strong passwords: Use different user ID / password combinations for different accounts and avoid writing them down. Make the passwords more complicated by combining letters, numbers, special characters (minimum 10 characters in total) and change them on a regular basis.
  3. Only open attachments from verified and trusted senders: Never open an attachment from a sender you do not know.
  4. Protect your personal information: Be cautious when giving out personal information such as your name, address, phone number or financial information on the Internet.
  5. Be careful with public Wi-Fi: Hackers can easily connect to public Wi-Fi and watch your every move, including what passwords and account information you enter while you’re connected. To keep your information safe, either don’t use public Wi-Fi or avoid entering private information and using apps that have passwords when you are on public Wi-Fi.
  6. Run an antivirus software and update it regularly: Using anti-virus or a comprehensive internet security solution is a smart way to protect your computer. Anti-virus software allows you to scan, detect and remove threats before they become a problem.
  7. Be cautious when clicking on links or downloading files: Cyber criminals can compromise your information through tweets, posts, and online advertising, in addition to emails. If it looks suspicious, just delete it. Beware of anyone who implores you to act immediately.

What is Digital Forensics?

  • Digital forensics is the practice of identifying, acquiring, and analyzing electronic evidence to investigate and solve crimes.
  • It involves the recovery, investigation, examination, and analysis of material found in digital devices, often in relation to mobile devices and computer crime.
  • Digital evidence is information stored or transmitted in binary form that may be relied on in court.
  • Digital forensics is important because almost all criminal activity has a digital forensics element, and digital forensics experts provide critical assistance to police investigations.
  • Digital forensic data is commonly used in court proceedings.
  • Digital forensics is useful in the aftermath of an attack to provide information required by auditors, legal teams, or law enforcement.

Explain different types of digital forensics

Explain process of digital forensics

  1. Identification: This involves identifying the devices and resources containing the data that may be relevant to the investigation.
  2. Preservation: In this phase, data is isolated, secured, and preserved to prevent tampering.
  3. Analysis: In this step, investigation agents reconstruct fragments of data and draw conclusions based on evidence found.
  4. Documentation: In this process, a record of all the visible data must be created.
  5. Presentation: Once the investigation is complete, the findings are presented to a court or the committee or group that will determine the outcome of a lawsuit or an internal investigation.

Explain incident response

Incident response is the process of handling and managing a cybersecurity incident or data breach.

The goal of incident response is to minimize the damage caused by the incident and to recover from it as quickly as possible.

The incident response process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Preparation: This involves developing an incident response plan, identifying the team responsible for incident response, and establishing procedures for detecting and reporting incidents.
  2. Detection and Analysis: This involves identifying and analyzing the incident to determine its scope, impact, and cause.
  3. Containment, Eradication, and Recovery: This involves containing the incident to prevent further damage, eradicating the cause of the incident, and restoring systems and data to their normal state.
  4. Post-Incident Activity: This involves reviewing the incident response process to identify areas for improvement and to prevent similar incidents from occurring in the future.

Benefits of Digital forensics

  • Preservation of Evidence: 
    • Digital forensics plays a crucial role in preserving evidence that can be used in court.
    • The process of collecting and analyzing digital evidence is done in a way that ensures its integrity and authenticity, making it admissible in court.
  • Identification of Criminals: 
    • Digital forensics can be used to identify the perpetrators of a crime.
    • This helps law enforcement agencies in their investigations and brings criminals to justice.
  • Protection of Corporate Interests: 
    • This helps organizations in maintaining the confidentiality of their data and protecting their reputation.
  • Assisting in Cybercrime Investigations: 
    • It can help in tracing the origin of a cyber-attack and identifying the source of a leak.
    • This helps law enforcement agencies in their investigations and brings cybercriminals to justice.
  • Facilitating Legal Proceedings: 
    • Digital forensics can assist in legal proceedings by providing evidence that can be used in court.
    • It can help in proving the authenticity of digital evidence and linking suspects to a crime scene.

Explain Anti-forensic

  • Anti-forensics refers to the techniques used to obstruct or prevent forensic analysis.
  • The goal of anti-forensics is to make it difficult or impossible for forensic investigators to retrieve evidence from a crime scene or to conduct analysis and examination of evidence.
  • Anti-forensic techniques include data hiding, artifact wiping, trail obfuscation, attacks against computer forensics processes and tools, and encryption.
  • Anti-forensic tools can be used to increase security, but they can also be misused by perpetrators of cybercrimes to hide their tracks.
  • Anti-forensic techniques can be used to hide the identity of the attacker or to make it difficult to trace the attack back to its source.
  • Anti-forensic techniques can be used to make it difficult to recover deleted files or to recover data from damaged or corrupted storage devices.
  • Anti-forensic techniques can be used to encrypt data or to hide data in plain sight.
  • Anti-forensic techniques can be used to make it difficult to recover data from network traffic or to trace network activity back to its source.

What are objectives of Digital forensics?

  • To identify, preserve, analyze, document, and present material found on digital or electronic devices, such as computers and smartphones.
  • To reconstruct past events and understand how and why they occurred.
  • To aid in criminal investigations by providing digital evidence that can be used in court.
  • To design procedures at a suspected crime scene that help ensure that the digital evidence obtained is not tainted.
  • To quickly identify evidence and estimate the potential impact of malicious activity on the victim.
  • To produce a computer forensic report that provides comprehensive information on the investigation process.
  • To ensure the integrity of the computer system.
  • To extract, process, and interpret factual evidence that proves the cybercriminal action's in the court.
  • To collect, preserve, and analyze digital evidence more effectively and efficiently.
  • To ensure that digital evidence is collected, analyzed, and preserved in a forensically sound manner, ensuring that it is admissible in court.
  • To create exact copies of digital media, including hard drives and other storage devices, without altering the original data.
