Certificate Authority

A Certificate Authority (CA) is a trusted third party that issues digital certificates to verify the identity of participants within the Hyperledger Fabric network.

  • Identity Verification: CAs verify the identities of network members (peers, orderers, clients) before issuing certificates.
  • Certificate Management: They handle the issuance, renewal, and revocation of digital certificates.
  • Enrollment: Participants must enroll with the CA to obtain certificates, proving their identity.

Chaincode in Hyperledger Fabric

Chaincode, similar to smart contracts, defines the business logic and rules for a Hyperledger Fabric blockchain network.

  • Business Logic: Chaincode dictates how ledger data is accessed and modified, automating complex processes.
  • Execution: It runs in a secured Docker container, isolated from the endorsing peer process.
  • Customizable: Chaincode can be written in Go, Node.js, or Java.

ERC20 Tokens in Blockchain Applications

ERC20 is a technical standard for fungible tokens on the Ethereum blockchain, ensuring assets function properly within the Ethereum ecosystem.

  • Interoperability: ERC20 tokens ensure compatibility between different assets and applications on the Ethereum blockchain.
  • Programmable Money: They facilitate the development of lending platforms, decentralized exchanges, and liquidity pools.
  • Representation of Assets: ERC20 tokens can represent real-world assets like real estate or artwork on the blockchain, increasing liquidity and fractional ownership.

What is forking in blockchain ? Explain hard and soft forks with suitable examples. (10 Marks, 10 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 May, 2023 May, 2023 December)

A blockchain fork happens when a blockchain splits into two or more separate chains.

This can occur in two main ways:

  • Accidental Forks
    • These are temporary splits, often due to network issues, like when two miners find a block at the same time. The network resolves this by choosing the longest chain, and the other chain is discarded.
  • Intentional Forks
    • These are planned changes to the blockchain‚Äôs rules. There are two types:
      • Soft Forks: These are upgrades where the new rules are a subset of the old ones, meaning old nodes can still accept new blocks. An example is Bitcoin‚Äôs Segregated Witness (SegWit) upgrade, which didn‚Äôt create a new chain.
      • Hard Forks: These are changes that aren‚Äôt backward compatible, creating a new chain separate from the old one. This can lead to a new cryptocurrency, like Bitcoin Cash, which split from Bitcoin in 2017 over block size disagreements.

What is a private blockchain ? Explain the distinct features and drawbacks of a private blockchain. Discuss any two examples of private blockchain. (10 Marks, 10 Marks) (2024 May, 2023 December)

A private blockchain, also known as a permissioned blockchain, is a blockchain network that requires authorization to participate. Unlike public blockchains, where anyone can join, a private blockchain is controlled by a single organization or a group of known entities. This central authority determines who can access the network, view the data, and participate in the transaction validation process.

Distinct Features of a Private Blockchain:

  • Permissioned Access: Only authorized participants can join and interact with the network.
  • Centralized Control: A single organization or a consortium governs the network and its rules.
  • Enhanced Privacy: Transaction data and network activity can be restricted to authorized participants.
  • Faster Transaction Speeds: With a smaller and known set of participants, consensus mechanisms can be more efficient, leading to faster transaction processing.

Drawbacks of a Private Blockchain:

  • Centralization: The centralized nature can be seen as a contradiction to the core principles of blockchain and may lead to a single point of failure or control.
  • Trust Requirement: Participants need to trust the central authority or governing entities.
  • Limited Transparency: The restricted access can reduce the transparency and immutability benefits typically associated with public blockchains.
  • Lower Security: Compared to large, decentralized public blockchains, a private blockchain with fewer nodes might be more vulnerable to attacks if the central authority is compromised.

Examples of Private Blockchain:

  1. Hyperledger Fabric: An open-source, enterprise-grade permissioned distributed ledger framework hosted by the Linux Foundation.
  2. Private Ethereum Blockchain: Unlike the public Ethereum mainnet, a private Ethereum blockchain restricts access to a select group of participants.
  3. R3 Corda: A permissioned distributed ledger technology designed specifically for businesses, particularly in the financial services industry.

Discuss Simplified Payment Verification (SPV) Nodes with its Privacy issue and solution. (10 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 December, 2023 May)

  • Lightweight nodes, also known as Simplified Payment Verification (SPV) nodes, download only block headers, not the entire blockchain.
  • They rely on full nodes for transaction verification.
  • Commonly used in mobile wallets and applications with limited storage and bandwidth.

Privacy Solutions for SPV Nodes

  1. Reduced Data Exposure:
    • SPV nodes use Merkle proofs to verify transactions.
    • They request only block headers and necessary Merkle branches, minimizing downloaded and stored data.
    • This limited data exposure enhances user privacy by not revealing the entire transaction history.
  2. Random Connections:
    • To prevent attacks like Denial of Service (DoS) or double-spending, SPV nodes connect randomly to different full nodes, increasing the chance of communicating with an honest node.
    • Connecting to a random set of nodes also reduces the risk of tracking by a single entity.
  3. Vulnerability Mitigation:
    • While vulnerable to attacks like network partitioning or Sybil attacks, SPV nodes mitigate some risks by connecting to multiple nodes for data and using Merkle proofs.
  4. Tor Network:
    • Communication between SPV and full nodes can reduce anonymity, causing privacy concerns.
  5. Bloom Filters:
    • SPV nodes can use Bloom filters to request transactions without revealing the exact transactions of interest, providing a layer of privacy.

Explain in Block in a Blockchain with terminologies as: Structure of a Block, Block Header Hash, Block Height, The Genesis Block, Linking Blocks in the Blockchain. (10 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 December, 2023 May)

A Block in a Blockchain is a fundamental data structure that stores a collection of transactions and metadata, forming a single unit in the blockchain's sequential chain.

Structure of a Block: A block is a data structure containing:

  • Block Header: Metadata about the block.
    • Previous Block: Hash of previous block.
    • Timestamp: Creation time of block in Unix timestamp format.
    • Nonce: A number miners adjust to find a valid hash for PoW.
    • Difficulty Target: The mining difficulty required for the block‚Äôs Proof of Work (PoW).
  • Transaction Data: List of transactions included in the block.
  • Other Data: Optional fields like block size or version.

Block Header Hash:

  • A unique cryptographic hash (e.g., SHA-256) of the block header.
  • Acts as the block's identifier.
  • Ensures integrity; any change in the block alters the hash.

Block Height:

  • The position of a block in the blockchain, starting from 0 (genesis block).
  • Example: The 100th block has a block height of 99.

The Genesis Block:

  • The first block in a blockchain (block height 0).
  • Hardcoded into the blockchain protocol.
  • Has no previous block hash, as it‚Äôs the starting point.

Linking Blocks in the Blockchain:

  • Blocks are linked in a chain via the previous block hash in the block header.
  • Each block references the hash of the previous block, forming a chronological sequence.
  • Ensures immutability; altering a block breaks the chain, as subsequent hashes mismatch.

Enumerate the interoperability and scalability issues in Hyperledger Fabric. (10 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 May, 2023 May)

Interoperability challenge

  • Interoperability refers to the ability of different blockchain networks to communicate and exchange data seamlessly.
  • However, achieving interoperability is challenging due to the following reasons:
    1. Lack of Standardization: Different blockchain platforms use various protocols, consensus mechanisms, and data formats, making it difficult for them to interact with each other.
    2. Complex Integration: Integrating blockchain with existing legacy systems requires significant effort and resources.
    3. Security Concerns: Ensuring secure data transfer between different blockchain networks is a major concern.

Scalability challenge

  • Scalability is the ability of a blockchain network to handle a growing amount of transactions and data.
  • The main challenges related to scalability include:
    1. Transaction Throughput: Traditional blockchain networks, like Bitcoin and Ethereum, have limited transaction throughput, leading to slow processing times and high fees during peak periods.
    2. Network Latency: As the number of nodes in a blockchain network increases, the time required to propagate transactions and blocks across the network also increases, resulting in higher latency.
    3. Energy Consumption: The consensus mechanisms used in many blockchain networks, such as Proof of Work (PoW), are energy-intensive and not sustainable for large-scale applications.

Bitcoin mining is the process by which

  • new bitcoins are created and
  • transactions are added to the blockchain.

Miners use computational power to solve complex cryptographic puzzles, and the first miner to solve the puzzle gets to add a new block to the blockchain and is rewarded with newly created bitcoins and transaction fees.

The Bitcoin mining process involves several key steps:

  • Transaction Validation: Nodes in the Bitcoin network validate transactions by checking digital signatures, ensuring inputs are unspent, and verifying that they follow protocol rules. ¬†
  • Inclusion in Blocks: Miners include these validated transactions in new blocks. ¬†
  • Proof of Work: Miners solve a complex Proof of Work puzzle to add the block to the blockchain. ¬†

What is nonce in blockchain and how does it work? (5 Marks, 10 Marks) (2023 May, 2023 December)

  • Definition: A "number only used once" that miners adjust to find a valid hash for a block in Proof of Work (PoW) systems.
  • Purpose:
    • Initially set by network software but modifiable by the miner.
    • Used to vary the input of the hash function, allowing miners to generate different hashes until the difficulty target is met.
    • Acts like a salt in hashing; the generated hash must match the difficulty rate format (e.g., 000x10xxxx000xxx).
  • Values:
    • Can be any integer. Miners increment the nonce and rehash the block header until a valid hash is found.
    • In Bitcoin, it's a 32-bit integer ranging from 0 to 4,294,967,295.

Transaction GAS (5 Marks, 10 Marks) (2024 December, 2023 December)

  • Unit of Computational Effort: Gas is a unit that measures the computational work required to execute operations on the Ethereum network.
  • Gas Limit: The maximum amount of gas a user is willing to pay for a transaction. If the gas consumed exceeds the limit, the transaction will fail (but the gas spent is still lost).
  • Gas Price: The amount of Ether (in Gwei, a smaller unit of Ether) a user is willing to pay per unit of gas.
  • Transaction Fee Calculation: Transaction Fee = Gas Used * Gas Price.
  • Purpose:
    • Preventing Spam: Makes it economically infeasible for malicious actors to overload the network with excessive computations.
    • Resource Allocation: Ensures that network resources are used efficiently and that users pay for the computational resources they consume.
    • Incentivizing Miners/Validators: Provides a reward for the computational work performed to process and secure transactions.

Explain CAP Theorem. (5 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 December, 2023 December)

The CAP Theorem, also known as Brewer's Theorem, states that in a distributed data store, it is impossible to simultaneously guarantee all three of the following properties: ¬†

  • Consistency: Every read receives the most recent write or an error. ¬†
  • Availability: Every request receives a response, without guarantee that it contains the most recent write.
  • Partition Tolerance: The system continues to operate despite an arbitrary number of messages being dropped or delayed by the network between nodes.

Byzantine Generals Problem Consensus Mechanism (5 Marks, 5 Marks) (2024 December, 2024 May)

A theoretical problem in distributed systems where¬†nodes must agree on a unified action¬†despite:

  • Malicious actors¬†(traitors) spreading conflicting information.
  • Unreliable communication¬†(messages delayed or lost).

Example:

Imagine generals surrounding a city, needing to coordinate an attack via messengers. Some generals are traitors who send false orders. The loyal generals must agree on a plan despite deception.

Relevance to Blockchain Consensus

Blockchains are decentralized networks where nodes must agree on transaction validity. The BGP highlights the need for¬†Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT)¬†to ensure consensus even if some nodes:

  • Lie¬†(e.g., double-spend attacks).
  • Fail¬†(e.g., go offline).

How Consensus Mechanisms Solve Byzantine General Problem

  1. Proof of Work (PoW)
    • Mechanism: Miners solve cryptographic puzzles to validate blocks.
    • BFT: Attackers need >51% of computational power to disrupt consensus.
  2. Proof of Stake (PoS)
    • Mechanism: Validators are chosen based on their staked tokens.
    • BFT: Malicious validators risk losing their stake ("slashing").

Blockchain technology can enhance the Internet of Things (IoT) by providing a secure and transparent way to manage data from IoT devices.

How Blockchain Integrates with IoT:

  1. Device Identity and Authentication: Each IoT device can have a unique identity recorded on the blockchain.
  2. Data Integrity and Security: Data collected by IoT devices can be recorded on the blockchain, ensuring its integrity and preventing tampering.
  3. Decentralized Data Sharing: Blockchain facilitates secure and transparent data sharing between authorized IoT devices and stakeholders without relying on a central intermediary. ¬†
  4. Smart Contracts for Automation: Smart contracts, self-executing agreements with the terms of the agreement directly written into code, can automate interactions and transactions between IoT devices based on predefined conditions. ¬†

  • Supply Chain Management: Tracking goods across the supply chain with IoT sensors and recording their location, temperature, and other relevant data on a blockchain provides transparency and accountability.

Describe the various components in Hyperledger Fabric. (10 Marks) (2024 December)

  • Peers: These are the fundamental nodes in the network that host ledgers and smart contracts (chaincode). They maintain a copy of the ledger and execute chaincode to process transactions. Peers can be:
    • Endorsing Peers: Specifically execute chaincode and endorse transaction proposals.
    • Committing Peers: Validate endorsed transactions and commit them to the ledger.
    • Anchor Peers: Facilitate cross-organization communication within a channel.
  • Ordering Service (Orderer): This component is responsible for ordering transactions into blocks and delivering these blocks to all peers in the network.
  • Channels: These are private subnetworks within the Fabric network that allow a specific set of participants to conduct private and confidential transactions.
  • Chaincode (Smart Contracts): Smart contracts in Hyperledger Fabric, written in Go, Java, or Node.js, that define the business logic of the application.
  • Ledger: This is the distributed and immutable record of all transactions in the network. It consists of two parts:
    • Blockchain (Chain): An ordered, tamper-proof sequence of blocks, where each block contains a batch of transactions.
    • World State: A database that holds the current value of all assets on the ledger.
  • Membership Service Provider (MSP): The MSP manages identities and defines the rules for who is allowed to participate in the network.

Explain the Proof of Stake (10 Marks) (2024 May)

Proof of Stake (PoS) is a consensus mechanism used to verify transactions on a blockchain. Instead of relying on the computational power of miners (as in Proof of Work), PoS selects validators to create new blocks based on the amount of cryptocurrency they "stake" or lock up.

  • Staking: Cryptocurrency holders can "stake" their coins, essentially locking them up as collateral.
  • Validators: These staked coins give the owners the chance to be chosen as validators.
  • Selection: The selection process for validators can vary but often prioritizes those with a larger stake. Some systems also consider the "age" of the staked coins.
  • Validation: Chosen validators verify new transactions and create new blocks.
  • Rewards: Validators are typically rewarded with transaction fees or newly minted cryptocurrency for their work.

Discuss the different consensus algorithms applied in Hyperledger blockchain. (10 Marks) (2024 May)

Solo

  • Type: Single ordering node.
  • Function: Transactions are ordered by this single node sequentially as they are received.
  • Fault Tolerance: None. If the solo node fails, the ordering service halts.
  • Use Case: Primarily for development and testing environments due to its simplicity. ¬†

Kafka

  • Type: Distributed ordering service based on Apache Kafka.
  • Fault Tolerance: Crash fault-tolerant (CFT) as long as a majority of Kafka brokers and ZooKeeper nodes are operational.
  • Use Case: Suitable for production networks requiring higher throughput and fault tolerance compared to Solo.
  • Note: The Kafka-based ordering service has been deprecated in later versions in favor of Raft.¬†

Raft

  • Type: Crash fault-tolerant (CFT) ordering service based on the Raft consensus protocol.
  • Function: Employs a leader-follower model where a leader is elected among the ordering nodes per channel, and its decisions are replicated to the followers. ¬†
  • Fault Tolerance: Can tolerate the loss of ordering nodes, including the leader, as long as a majority (quorum) of nodes remain.
  • Use Case: Recommended for production networks due to its ease of setup, management, and crash fault tolerance. It also supports dynamic membership.

What is difficulty level and how does it calculate in terms of bitcoin (10 Marks) (2023 December)

Difficulty Level in Bitcoin:

The difficulty level in Bitcoin is a measure of how hard it is to find a new block compared to the easiest it can ever be. It essentially quantifies the computational resources required to mine a valid block.

  • A higher difficulty means that miners need to perform more hashing operations on average to find a hash that meets the target.
  • A lower difficulty means it's easier to find a valid block.

Calculation of Difficulty Level:

The difficulty level is not directly calculated in terms of a specific Bitcoin amount but is derived from the target hash. The target hash is a 256-bit number that the block's hash must be less than or equal to for the block to be considered valid.

The difficulty is calculated using the following formula:

$$Difficulty={\text{Current¬†Target} \over \text{Maximum¬†Target}}$$‚Äã

  • Maximum Target: This is a fixed 256-bit number that represents the easiest possible mining difficulty (difficulty of 1).
  • Current Target: This is the target hash for the most recent block. It is a 256-bit number that is adjusted by the Bitcoin network.

Key Generation

  • The public key is a point on the elliptic curve obtained by multiplying the private key with a predefined point on the curve (called the base point):

$$ \text{Private Key} \times \text{Base Point} = \text{Public Key} $$

Properties of Elliptic Curves (Relevant to Cryptography)

  • Symmetric to the x-axis.
  • A non-vertical line drawn from any point intersects the curve at no more than three points.
  • Points on the curve can be "added" together to produce another point on the curve.

  • However, given the public key (B) and the base point (A), it is computationally infeasible to find the private key (k). This is the basis of the trapdoor function in ECC.

A Merkle Tree is a hierarchical data structure used to verify the integrity of data efficiently. Here's how it is constructed:

  • Step 1: Leaf Nodes
    Each data block (e.g., transactions in a blockchain) is hashed individually using a cryptographic hash function (e.g., SHA-256).
  • Step 2: Pairing and Hashing
    • Even Number of Leaves: Pair adjacent hashes and hash them together.
    • Odd Number of Leaves: Duplicate the last hash to form a pair.
  • Step 3: Building the Tree
    Repeat the pairing process recursively until a single root hash remains:
    • Combine parent nodes to form higher-level nodes.
    • Final root hash is the¬†Merkle Root.

Merkle Root: Usage and Importance

  • Data Integrity
    • Any alteration to a transaction (e.g., changing L1) changes its hash, propagating up the tree and altering the Merkle Root.
    • The Merkle Root is embedded in the¬†block header¬†(e.g., in Bitcoin), making tampering evident.
  • Efficient Verification
    • Nodes can verify the inclusion of a transaction without downloading the entire dataset.
    • Used in¬†Simplified Payment Verification (SPV)¬†for lightweight clients (e.g., mobile wallets).

    • Bitcoins are sent to a script hash, allowing for more complex spending conditions like multisignature.

What is a Segregated Witness in a Transaction and how does it help to increase the Transaction Per Second (TPS)?

  • Definition:
    • Introduced in 2017 via BIP 141
    • Segregated Witness (SegWit) separates the "witness" data (signatures and public keys) from the main transaction data (inputs, outputs, etc.).
    • This witness data is stored in a separate section of the block, not counted fully toward the traditional 1 MB block size limit.
  • Impact on TPS:
    • Increased Block Capacity: By segregating witness data, SegWit effectively raises the block size limit to approximately 4 MB.
    • Smaller Transactions: Removing signatures from the main data reduces transaction size, allowing more to fit in each block.
    • Higher Throughput: While Bitcoin‚Äôs base TPS is around 7 (due to 10-minute block intervals), SegWit increases on-chain capacity and supports off-chain scaling, indirectly raising the effective TPS.
    • Lightning Network

Example: A pre-SegWit block might fit 2,000 transactions, while a SegWit block could fit 3,000+, enhancing throughput and supporting scalable solutions.
